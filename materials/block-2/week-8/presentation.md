### Certification Threshold

**22 points minimum required for certification**

**Speaker Notes:**
Most participants land in the 28-35 range. The goal is honest self-assessment, not maximum scores. Below 22 requires revision before proceeding to Block 3.

**BACKGROUND:**

**Rationale:**
- Clear scoring thresholds remove ambiguity about what constitutes successful completion
- The 22-point minimum (55%) represents competency - demonstrating fundamental capability without requiring perfection
- The distribution (28-35 typical) sets realistic expectations and reduces anxiety about achieving maximum scores

**Key Research & Citations:**
- **Competency-Based Assessment**: Research shows that minimum competency thresholds (50-60% of maximum) effectively distinguish capable practitioners from those needing additional development, while avoiding false precision of higher thresholds
- **Self-Assessment Calibration**: Studies of professional self-assessment show that clear rubrics with examples improve assessment accuracy by 40-60% compared to vague criteria
- **Certification Standards**: Professional certifications typically require 60-75% for passing. Block 2's 55% threshold acknowledges that this is foundational learning, not expert certification

**Q&A Preparation:**
- *"Why is 22/40 (55%) the passing threshold?"*: Block 2 is foundational - we're looking for competency, not mastery. 55% demonstrates you've built working systems and understand core concepts. Higher thresholds would require expertise that comes with practice beyond this program.
- *"What happens if I score below 22?"*: You'll receive specific feedback on gaps and opportunity to revise and resubmit. The goal is learning, not gatekeeping. Most revisions successfully achieve certification.
- *"Can I proceed to Block 3 with a score of 22-27?"*: Yes - that range indicates adequate competency. Block 3 will build on these foundations. A 22 means you've demonstrated minimum capability; a 27 means you're solidly competent.

**Sources:**
- Competency-Based Education Research - CAEL, 2023
- Self-Assessment in Professional Development - Harvard Education Review, 2022

## Slide 6: Self-Evaluation Tips

### How to Assess Honestly

**Do:**
- Provide specific evidence for each score
- Reference actual files and outputs
- Be realistic about gaps
- Note what you'd improve

**Don't:**
- Inflate scores without evidence
- Be overly harsh on yourself
- Skip criteria
- Rush through

### The Goal

Accurate self-assessment is a career skill.
Learning to evaluate your own work honestly makes you better.

**Speaker Notes:**
Self-evaluation accuracy matters more than high scores. Inflated scores without evidence are obvious. Honest assessment with evidence of growth is valued.

**BACKGROUND:**

**Rationale:**
- Self-evaluation is a critical professional skill that many participants have never practiced formally
- The slide establishes that honest self-assessment demonstrates maturity, not weakness
- Providing clear Do/Don't guidance removes ambiguity and anxiety about the evaluation process

**Key Research & Citations:**
- **Metacognitive Research**: Studies show that accurate self-assessment correlates with professional growth. Individuals who can honestly evaluate their work improve faster than those who inflate or deflate their abilities.
- **Growth Mindset Literature (Dweck)**: Honest acknowledgment of gaps is essential for improvement. Fixed mindset sees gaps as failures; growth mindset sees them as learning opportunities.
- **Professional Development Best Practices**: High-performing organizations emphasize self-assessment accuracy over score maximization in evaluation processes. Calibration (honest self-view) predicts future performance.

**Q&A Preparation:**
- *"What if I think I deserve a 5 but can't prove it?"*: Then it's probably a 4. The evidence requirement isn't arbitrary - it's what makes self-evaluation credible. If you can't show why it's excellent, reassess.
- *"Should I be harsh on myself to look modest?"*: No - be accurate. False modesty is as problematic as false confidence. If you met requirements well, give yourself a 4. If you exceeded them, give yourself a 5. Evidence supports both.
- *"What if I disagree with the rubric criteria?"*: Note your disagreement in reflection, but score against the stated criteria. Professional growth includes working within given frameworks, even when you'd design them differently.

### Appendix F: Common Self-Evaluation Mistakes

**Inflation Without Evidence:**
- Claiming "5" but only providing basic examples
- *Fix:* Match score to evidence. If evidence is basic, score is 3-4, not 5.

**Excessive Modesty:**
- Giving "2" when work clearly meets requirements
- *Fix:* If you met requirements with evidence, you've earned at least 3.

**Vague Evidence:**
- "My workflows work well" without specifics
- *Fix:* Provide concrete examples: "Workflow executed 47 times with 96% success rate"

**Missing Evidence:**
- Scoring without providing supporting links/examples
- *Fix:* Every score needs evidence. Link to files, provide examples, show results.

**Ignoring Gaps:**
- Scoring "4" while acknowledging major gaps
- *Fix:* Gaps reduce scores. Acknowledge them honestly and score accordingly.

**Comparing to Others:**
- "Mine isn't as good as X's work"
- *Fix:* Score against rubric, not peers. Different use cases = different complexity.
